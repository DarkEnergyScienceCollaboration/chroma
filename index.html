LSST will measure the shapes of some billion galaxies to constrain cosmic shear and dark energy. With this unprecedented statistical precision, a deep understanding of potential systematic biases in will be required. In this LSST DESC feature project, we investigate biases originating from two chromatic effects in the atmosphere: (i) differential chromatic refraction (DCR) and (ii) wavelength dependence of seeing. These biases arise when using the point spread function (PSF) measured with stars to estimate the shapes of galaxies with different spectral energy distributions (SEDs) than the stars.

Differential Chromatic Refraction

DCR is the effect that bluer photons are bent slightly more toward the zenith when entering the atmosphere than are redder photons (Figure 1). This leads to both an SED-dependent shift in zenith-direction PSF centroid and a contraction of the PSF in the zenith direction. These effects can be quantified in terms of the shifts in the PSF zenith-direction first and second moments, denoted \(\bar{R}\) and \(V\), respectively.

Chromatic Seeing

The atmosphere smears out blue photons somewhat more than red photons, in the sense that there is a relationship \(FWHM \propto \lambda^{-1/5}\). This effect can be characterized as an SED-dependent shift in the second moment square radius \(r^2\), which is equal to the sum of the second moments measured in perpendicular directions, and scales like \(\lambda^{-2/5}\) (see Figure 1).

Induced shape calibration biases

It is common to characterize systematic uncertainties in shape measurement in terms of the additive and multiplicative calibration parameters \(m\) and \(c\), define by \[\hat{\gamma_i} = (1+m_i)\gamma_i + c_i.\] where \(\hat{\gamma_i}\) is the shear estimate for the true shear \(\gamma_i\). In these terms, the bias from differential chromatic refraction can be written \[m_1^{DCR} = m_2^{DCR} = \frac{- \Delta V}{r^2_{gal}} \] \[c_1^{DCR} = \frac{- \Delta V}{2r^2_{gal}} \] \[c_2^{DCR} = 0.\] where \(\Delta V = V_{PSF, star} - V_{PSF, gal}\), and \(r^2_{gal}\) is the second moment square radius of the galaxy under consideration. Similarly, the bias for chromatic seeing becomes \[m_1^{seeing} = m_2^{seeing} = \frac{-r^2_{psf}}{r^2_{gal}}\frac{\Delta r^2_{psf}}{r^2_{psf}}\] \[c_i^{seeing} = -\epsilon_i^{psf}\frac{r^2_{psf}}{r^2_{gal}}\frac{\Delta r^2_{psf}}{r^2_{psf}}.\] where \(\Delta r^2_{psf} = r^2_{PSF, star} - r^2_{PSF, gal}\) and \(\epsilon_i^{psf}\) is the (usually small) ellipticity of the PSF. For LSST, the requirements are approximately to keep \(|m|\) and \(|c|\) below 0.003 and 0.0006, respectively.

Analysis

Our analysis consists of three parts:

  • Using galaxy and stellar catalogs generated from the LSST catalogs framework, we make analytic estimates of the sizes of the two chromatic biases.
  • With the same catalogs, we also investigate techniques to predict the size of chromatic biases from LSST multi-filter photometry so that they can be removed.
  • From simulated images, we also demonstrate an additional “model bias” arising from the unknown shape of the point spread function.

In addition to its relevance to LSST, these analyses are applicable to other large-volume ground-based surveys such as DES, Hyper Suprime-Cam and KIDS, where the same atmospheric biases will manifest. The wavelength dependence of seeing present for atmospheric turbulence is also qualitatively similar to the wavelength dependence of the diffraction limit, which will affect space-based cosmic shear surveys such as Euclid.
